Brownfield Land Register

The Government is committed to maximising the number of new homes built on suitable brownfield land and has set out its intention to ensure that 90 per cent of suitable brownfield sites have planning permission for housing by 2020.

The Housing and Planning Act, 2016 (opens in a new window) makes provision for local authorities to prepare, maintain and publish a register of brownfield land. The register should identify previously developed sites in the district that we have assessed as being suitable for housing.

Further information can be found within national Planning Practice Guidance regarding the Brownfield Land Register (opens in a new window)and Permission in Principle (opens in a new window).

The Brownfield Register can be downloaded and viewed here: Brownfield Land Register [22.68KB] (opens in a new window) (Last Modified: 23 February 2018)

* If the land is entered in Part 2 of the Brownfield Land Register it will be granted permission in principle, which establishes the suitability, in principle, of land for housing-led development.

Information regarding all the sites will be updated in the following PDF document following each stage (i.e. Planning Committee, consultation etc.): Brownfield Land Sites [120.41KB] (opens in a new window)

What is Brownfield Land (Also known as Previously Developed Land)?

Previously developed land is defined in the NPPF as: "Land which is or was occupied by a permanent structure, including the curtilage of the developed land (although it should not be assumed that the whole of the curtilage should be developed) and any associatedfixed surface infrastructure. This excludes: land that is or has been occupied by agricultural or forestry buildings; land that has been developed for minerals extraction or waste disposal by landfill purposes where provision for restoration has been made through development control procedures; land in built-up areas such as private residential gardens, parks, recreation grounds and allotments; and land that was previously-developed but where the remains of the permanent structure or fixed surface structure have blended into the landscape in the process of time."

What sites are eligible to be placed on the register?

Brownfield land sites above 0.25ha or those capable of providing for 5 or more dwellings considered to be suitable, available and achievable.

Suitable: Has been allocated in a local development plan document for residential development; has planning permission for residential development; has a grant of permission in principle for residential development; or, is in the opinion of the local planning authority, appropriate for residential development, having regard to -

Any adverse impact on -

  1. The natural environment;

  2. the local built environment, including in particular on heritage assets;

  3. any adverse impact on the local amenity which such development might cause for intended occupiers of the development or for occupiers of neighbouring properties; and

  4. any relevant representations received.

Available: The relevant owner (owners)/ developer, has expressed an intention to sell or develop the land at a date not more than 21 days before the entry date there I no evidence indicating a change to that intention. in the opinion of the local authority there are no issues relating to the ownership of the land or other legal impediments which might prevent residential development of the land taking place, having regard to -

  1. Any information publicly available on that date; and

  2. any relevant representations received.

Achievable: The development is likely to take place within 15 years of the entry date, having regard to -

  1. Any information publicly available; and

  2. any relevant representations received.

What sites are eligible for Part 2 of the register?

Sites will be included in Part 2 of the register where:

  • The requirements of a site being on Part 1 of the register have been met; and

  • The authority has decided to allocate the land residential development, having followed the publicity and notification (regulations 6 to 9) and consultation (regulations 10 to 13) procedures of the Brownfield Land Register Regulations, 2017.

How were sites initially added to the register?

The initial register was compiled from brownfield sites that meet the criteria as set out in the regulations. These included:

  • Sites that received a positive assessment through the Strategic Housing Land Availability Assessment (SHLAA);

  • Sites submitted through the Local Plan site selection process;

  • Sites were subject to a live planning permission granted within the past three years (01/04/2014 - 31/08/2017); and

  • Sites allocated through the Site Specific Policies & Proposals Development Plan Document.

Where sites were only partially on brownfield land only the part included on brownfield land was included within the register.

What is Permission in Principle?

The Permission in Principle (PIP) consent route is an alternative way of obtaining planning permission which separates the consideration of matters of principle for proposed development form the technical detail of the development.

PIP is available for housing or housing-led development on suitable sites entered on brownfield registers only. The scope of permission in principle is limited to location, land use and amount of development.

What is expected from the Technical Details Consent?

Following a grant of permission in principle, the site must receive a grant of technical details consent before development can proceed. The granting of technical details consent has the effect of granting planning permission for the development and other statutory requirements may apply at this stage e.g. relating to protected species or listed buildings. Technical details consent can be obtained following submission of a valid application to the local planning authority. An application for technical details consent must be in accordance with the permission in principle that is specified by the applicant (e.g. by giving the reference number from the planning register) in the technical details consent application form.

Can conditions be attached to Permission in Principle or Technical Details Consent applications?

Planning Practice Guidance states that "it is not possible for conditions to be attached to a Permission in Principle and its terms only include the site location, the type of development and amount of development. Local Planning authorities can inform applicants about what they expect to see at the technical details consent stage". However, conditions can be attached to a technical details consent.

Planning Fees?

  National Planning Practice Guidance states that a fee is payable for Technical Details Consent.

 

What is the consultation process?

A 42 day consultation period would be undertaken prior to a site being granted Permission in Principle. A further consultation period will be held on the Technical Details Consent in line with planning practice guidance, which states that this will be not less than 21 days for the site notice and 14 days for the online notice.
